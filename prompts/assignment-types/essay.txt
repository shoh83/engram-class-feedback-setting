Use an essay or paragraph-based rubric.
Evaluate the writing with these categories:
1. Task completion
2. Content and delivery
3. Organization and structure
4. Vocabulary and expression
5. Grammar accuracy

For essay writing, judge whether ideas develop across sentences into a coherent paragraph or multi-paragraph response.
Pay attention to paragraph structure, sentence-to-sentence reasoning, and how supporting details build the main point.

When grades or graded category judgments are needed, use this rubric and map it to these exact category names:
1. Task Completion
2. Content and Delivery
3. Organization and Structure
4. Vocabulary and Expression
5. Grammatical Accuracy

1. Task Completion
- A: Successfully addresses the topic and fulfills requirements. Minor omissions are acceptable.
- B: Mostly addresses the topic with 1-2 slight deviations.
- C: Addresses the topic but lacks detail or misses a key instruction.
- D: Only partially on-topic.
- E: Completely off-topic.

2. Content and Delivery
- A: Clear reasoning supported by relevant examples or details.
- B: Claims are easy to understand; logic is sound and easy to follow.
- C: Main point is visible, though supporting details are thin or vague.
- D: Vague or inconsistent; reasoning is thin or hard to follow.
- E: Minimal content; lacks a logical progression of ideas.

3. Organization and Structure
- A: Clear paragraph structure such as introduction-body-conclusion or another logical paragraph sequence. Sentences build on one another with smooth reasoning and transitions.
- B: The paragraph sequence is logical, and sentence-to-sentence connections are mostly easy to follow.
- C: The structure is understandable, but paragraph development or sentence links may feel repetitive, abrupt, or thin.
- D: Paragraph structure is weak, and reasoning between sentences is often uneven or confusing.
- E: The writing is disorganized, with little paragraph control or coherent connection between sentences.

4. Vocabulary and Expression
- A: Effective and appropriate word choice with some variety.
- B: Mostly appropriate word choice, though repetition may appear.
- C: Limited vocabulary with some awkwardness.
- D: Very limited vocabulary; frequent incorrect usage makes meaning hard to follow.
- E: Extremely limited and inappropriate language prevents communication.

5. Grammatical Accuracy
- A: Generally accurate; some errors are present but do not distract the reader significantly.
- B: Errors are somewhat frequent and slightly interfere with meaning.
- C: Persistent errors require the reader to slow down.
- D: Severe and frequent errors make parts of the text difficult to read.
- E: Errors are so extensive that the message becomes unintelligible.
